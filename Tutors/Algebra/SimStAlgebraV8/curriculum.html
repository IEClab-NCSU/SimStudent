Linear Equations with Variables on Both Sides

General Introduction

In this unit, you will learn how to solve linear equations, the hardest equations will have variables on both sides of the equation, for example, -2x-6 = 4+3x.

Key Terms

Important words and definitions:

  • Term: In the equation -2x+3 = 3x+5-7x, there are five terms, which are "-2x", "3", "3x", "5", and "-7x". There are two types of terms, constant terms and variable terms.
    • A constant term does not have a letter. Some examples are 3,-3,16,-12, 1/2. In the equation above, the "3" and "5" are constant terms
    • A variable term has a letter, Some examples are x,3x,-3b,-7c. In the equation above, "-2x", "3x" and "-7x" are variable terms.
    Notice that the minus sign is a part of the term: the last term in "3x+5-7x" is "-7x", not "7x".
    Also notice that you don't have to include the plus sign for the 3 or the 5: it is understood that terms without a sign are positive. Therefore, the second term on the left-hand side can be written as 3 or +3.

  • Coefficient: A coefficient is the number associated with a variable term.
    • the coefficient of the variable term 2y is 2
    • the coefficient of the variable term -4a is -4
    • the coefficient of the variable term 3x/4 is 3/4
  • Like terms: Like terms are terms that have identical variables and exponents.
    • Constant terms are always like terms with each other.
      • 2 and 7 are like terms
      • 14 and 35 are like terms
      • -3 and 5 are like terms
      • 1/3 and 7 are like terms
    • Variable terms are only like terms if the variables are the same (and the exponents are the same)
      • x and 2x are like terms
      • 3b and 4b are like terms
      • 3x and -2x are like terms
      • x/2 and x/7 are like terms
      • in the expression 3c-2+5-4c, "3c" and "-4c" are like terms; "-2" and "+5" are like terms.
      • 3b and 3x are NOT like terms because the variable letters do not match
      • 4 and 2x are NOT like terms because 4 is a constant term and 2x is a variable term
  • Fractions: On the computer, it is common to use the letter '/' to represent a fraction. So, 3a/3 is 3a over 3, which means 3a divided by 3.

Example Solutions

To solve an equation you need to do mathematical transformations until at the end you have "x=a number", for example x=-2. To solve a complex equation, you may need to do multiple transformations.

What is a "transformation"? Let's think about how to solve the following example:

  • -2x-6 = 4+3x
What does this equation mean? Yes, it means that -2x-6 and 4+3x are equal. In other words, "multiplying -2 and x and subtracting 6 from it" is equal to "the sum of 4 and 3 times x". Now, think about the following question. If -2x-6 and 4+3x are equal, then which of the following is bigger: -2x-6+6 or 4+3x+6? Yes, they are the same!! So, this means the following:
  • -2x-6+6 = 4+3x+6
What does this tell you? This tells you that if you have an equation (i.e., -2x-6=4+3x), adding the same number (i.e., 6) to both sides would maintain the equivalence! 'So what?' you ask? Look at the above equation carefully. What will you get if you combine like terms (which are -6 and 6) on the left-hand side? Yes, only -2x!
  • -2x=4+3x+6
After combining like terms (which are 4 and 6), the right hand-side becomes 10+3x
  • -2x=10+3x
Okay, let's summarize what was going on. You wanted to solve the following equation:
  • -2x-6 = 4+3x
So, you added 6 to both sides (because it would keep the equivalence of the equation):
  • -2x-6 = 4+3x add 6 (to both sides)
As a consequence, the original equation is now transformed into the following equation:
  • -2x = 10+3x

If you compare the two equations, -2x-6=4+3x and -2x=10+3x, you would notice that by adding 6 to both sides, you have eliminated the constant term of -6 from the left-hand side.

Easy, isn't it? This is how you solve equations -- equation solving is eliminating terms one at time and transforming the equation into simpler one.

Addition is not the only operation for a transformation. You can subtract, multiply, or divide. You can also add (or subtract, or multiply, or divide) a variable term. Indeed, for the above equation, you may want to subtract 3x from both sides:

  • -2x = 10+3x subtract 3x (from both sides)
Not sure why? What would you get if you subtract 3x from -2x? Yes, -5x. What about subtracting 3x from 10+3x? Yes, you'd get 10. As a result you get the following equation, in which the variable term '3x' on the right-hand side has been eliminated:
  • -5x = 10
Now, there is one last step to solve this equation. You may want to divide both sides by -5, because -5x divided by -5 would be x:
  • -5x = 10 divide (both sides by) -5
which results in the following equation:
  • x = -2
In summary, to solve the equation -2x-6 = 4+3x, you applied three transformations:

-2x-6 = 4+3x add 6
-2x = 10+3x subtract 3x
-5x = 10 divide -5
x = -2

Another Example

Let's review how to solve the equation 3p-8=10

Equation Transformation Explanation
3p-8 = 10 add 8 You may first add 8 to both sides. Why? Because if you add 8 to both sides, then the left-hand side of the resulting equation would be 3p.
3p = 18 divide 3 Now, you may want to divide both sides by 3, which is the coefficient of 3p.
p = 6 Problem Solved! Of course, a solution can be a fraction!!

So the solution to the equation is p=6.

Verifying the Solution

To verify that a solution is correct, substitute the solution into the original equation and see if the value of the left-hand side is equal to the value of the right-hand side. If you substitute p with 6 for 3p-8, you get 3*6-8, which is 10. This value is equal to the value of the right-hand side. So, p=6 is a correct solution of the original equation.


One More Example

What about equations like 3 = 4/a? This equation looks tricky, because the variable is the denominator of the fraction.

Equation Transformation Explanation
3=4/a multiply a You may want to multiply both sides by a. That way, the right-hand side would become 4 and the left-hand side will be 3a.
3a=4 divide 3 Now, the equation looks familiar. You would divide both sides by 3, wouldn't you?
a=4/3 Problem Solved!

Suggested Problems

When you tutor your student, you are welcome to make up your own problems. Here are some suggested problems for you to try out if you can't think of any.

Single-Step Equations - You can solve each of these equations using only one transformation each.

  • 5n=10
  • a+4=6

    • Two-Step Equations - These problems need two transformations to solve.

  • 2x+8=10
  • 3y-5=-2
  • 2z-7=1
  • 4=9x+3
  • 4=-3n+13
  • -b-6=5

    • Equations with Variables on Both Sides - These equations are the focus of this unit. These equations need more than two transformations.

  • 4-z=5z+16
  • 5x+3=4x+10
  • 3y-3=3+y
  • 6c+8=2c+3
  • 2x+3=x-5

    • You can also try changing these problems slightly. You might try:

  • Swapping negatives and positives.
  • Changing the order in which terms appear.
  • Adjusting the coefficients so that a different one is largest.
  • Using different constants.


    • Skills

    In this unit you will learn to:

    • Add to both sides of an equation: you can add either a constant term or a variable term to both sides of an equation.
      • Example: for the equation 2x-3=5, to eliminate "-3" on the left-hand side, you can "add 3" to both sides.
      • Example: for the equation 12-4x=2x, to eliminate "4x" on the left-hand side, you can "add 4x" to both sides
    • Subtract from both sides of an equation: you can subtract either a constant term or a variable term from both sides of an equation.
      • Example: for the equation -6+2x=-5x, to eliminate "2x" on the left-hand side, you can "subtract 2x" from both sides.
      • Example: for the equation 3+4x=5, to eliminate "3" on the left-hand side, you can "subtract 3" from both sides
    • Multiply both sides of an equation: you can multiply both sides of an equation by either a variable term or a constant term.
      • Example: for the equation 2/x=10, you can multiply both sides of the equation by "x" to eliminate x from the denominator fo the fraction on the left-hand side term. The result would be "2=10x".
      • Example: for the equation (x+3)/4=3, you can multiply both sides of the equation by "4" to eliminate 4 from the denominator of the left-hand side term. The result would be "x+3=12".
    • Divide both sides of an equation: you may divide both sides of an equation by a constant term.
      • Example: you can divide both sides of the equation -3x=12 by -3. The result would be "x=-4"
      • Example: you can divide both sides of the equation 2x=4 by 2. The result would be "x=2"
    • Combine like terms: Like terms in an equation can be combined.
      • Example: for the equation -3+2y+7 = 5y-3+y., -3 and 7 on the left-hand side become 4 when combined (-3+7 is equal to 4). On the right hand side, 5y and y become 6y when combined (5y+y is 6y). As a result of combining like terms, the equation -3+2y+7=5y-3+y becomes 4+2y=6y-3.
      • Example: for the equation 6x+4=2x+16, after you combine like terms (across the equal sign) it becomes 4x=12.
